### Candlestick Basics  (youtube.com/watch?v=WHkA0yEcJBw)
We are going to be going over the trading basics full breakdown just in case some of you guys are starting from zero knowledge of trading just like I did. So the candlestick basics: you're going to have a bullish candle and a bearish candle. The only thing that these things mean — these are indicators of where price is going. So let's say price opened down here and this is a dollar and it ended up closing at $2. But during this time frame, it ended up hitting $2.50 at its highest point of it being open, but it ended up actually closing at $2. Each one of these candles represents a time frame. So let's say this green candle represents 60 seconds of price movement — just one minute. So this opened and now we are at 0 seconds. Let's say we hit the high of $2.50 at 30 seconds and then we slowly went down and at the 60-second mark (the one-minute mark) we closed at $2 flat. That is exactly how these candles work. And on the opposite end, the bearish candlestick works the exact same way, just going down. So let's say we open up here and then the price goes lower, lower, lower. And let's say we're at 45 seconds out of the 60 when we're down here. And in that last couple seconds before it hits one minute, price went higher and closed here at that 60-second mark. So again, a green candle means price went up. A red candle means price went down.

### Market Structure Fundamentals  (youtube.com/watch?v=hLhU9hUC6Zc)
Market structure fundamentals — studying a bullish uptrend. A market is bullish when price is making higher highs and higher lows. This means buyers are in control, pushing price upward in waves. This is where we look for long/buy setups. You recognize a bullish uptrend by higher highs and higher lows (or just seeing the chart go up). A higher high: a high, then higher, then higher — peaks going higher and higher, paired with higher lows going up each time to match. A bearish downtrend (market going down) is created by lower highs and lower lows. This means sellers are dominating and driving the market downward — this is where you look for short/sell setups. The bearish version is the same thing, just opposite: a high, then a lower high, lower, keeping the downtrend; same for the lows (low, lower low, even lower low). Consolidation / range: price moves sideways between support and resistance; no clear trend; algorithms accumulate or distribute orders here. These are low-quality trading areas and we avoid them as much as possible. A consolidation looks like: price opens, moves up, dumps back down, tries to go lower but can't, goes up, tries a big move up, gets rejected back down; the low matches the level of the prior low — it tries to break a new low and cannot, gets rejected back up — like a ping-pong ball bouncing between two walls. The resistance line won't let price push up past it; the support line won't let price fall below it. Consolidation periods don't last forever — when a breakout happens, that means a big move in the market just occurred.

### Fair Value Gaps & Imbalances  (youtube.com/watch?v=zKf1g4Bigi0)
Fair value gaps are probably one of the most important things to understand for the strategy to work. A fair value gap (FVG) happens when price moves so fast that it does not fully trade through a price range, creating a three-candle pattern: candle A, candle B, candle C. If candle B's body (the middle) and wick do not overlap with candle A's wick, it creates an imbalance. The reason there's a gap is because the entirety of candle A does not touch candle C — it leaves a gap inside. Same concept to the downside. This gap indicates an imbalance: a gap going up = more buyers than sellers; a gap going down = more sellers than buyers. This is what we build the strategy around. The FVG shows price moved too fast and left unfilled orders; it means algorithms will return to fill the imbalance. The fill can act as a high-probability entry (for other people, not necessarily us). FVGs show where institutions (banks) stepped in aggressively — a huge order creates a gap. Key thing from day one: the market likes to create the gap, trade above it (if bullish/green), and revisit. We create a gap, trade below it, then rise back and revisit it. Most times it won't happen that quick unless it's a perfect setup, but it'll trend below and then revisit the gap ~99% of the time. Same opposite for a bearish gap: gap on a green candle, trade above the gap, revisit ~99% of the time.

### Support & Resistance Levels  (youtube.com/watch?v=GuAoSn3NpMA)
Support is a price level where buyers keep stepping in. Resistance is a price level where sellers keep stepping in. The algorithms target these for liquidity, stop hunts, reversals and continuation. When price can't budge above the resistance line, sellers are shorting/selling positions to force the market down. On the opposite side, when price can't break through the support line and continue down, that means people are buying more positions to make price/the market go up.

### Stop Loss & Take Profit  (youtube.com/watch?v=r-mFg7JOubs)
A stop-loss is a price level you preset to exit a losing trade automatically to protect capital — cutting losses early ("if price hits this, take me out"). The take-profit is the opposite: a preset price to close your trade in profit; it locks in gains and removes emotion ("when price hits this profit level, exit me and take the money"). **My strategy uses a stop-loss BELOW the fair value gap, and a take-profit 1–2 bullish candles above (riding the trend continuation).** (This makes more sense in the advanced videos.) Example: we buy in, want the market up; if it doesn't go, our stop-loss is where we take the loss early and cut off. Opposite: if the market goes up and hits our take-profit level, we take profit and cut off the risk of it dropping and losing the gains.

### Intro To Liquidity  (youtu.be/GL9HxdI2omA)
Purpose: understand WHY price goes where it goes, not follow blindly. By the end you should be able to explain what liquidity is, identify where it forms, understand why price targets certain highs/lows, and stop seeing the market as random.

**What is liquidity:** orders resting in the market — mostly people's stop-losses, pending orders, or breakout traders entering late. These orders cluster in obvious locations, making them attractive targets for large banks/corporations to push price into (up or down). Liquidity is the FUEL for the market — without it price can't move efficiently.

**Where liquidity forms:** where many traders think the same way — equal highs/equal lows, previous day highs/lows, range highs/lows, obvious support/resistance levels (including 4H/1H fair value gaps). When many traders place stops in the same area, that area becomes a liquidity pool.

**Buy-side vs sell-side:** Buy-side liquidity is above the highs (of day/week/month) — from buy stops, breakout buyers, short traders' stop-losses; price must move UP to access it. Sell-side liquidity is the same but at the bottom/lows; price must move DOWN to access it.

**Use:** We use liquidity for entries and especially EXITS. Take-profit is ~99% determined by where liquidity rests. A liquidity level is a high or low where price hasn't revisited yet (price loves to revisit). Stop-loss is placed at sell-side liquidity (an untested swing low) — even if price draws down, it's very unlikely to go below that swing low (with other confirmations pointing bullish).

**Two things price does at liquidity:** (1) run straight through it and keep going, or (2) "wick out" the liquidity — a liquidity grab: long wick clears the level but the candle does NOT close beyond it, then reverses. Can't predict which without more info.

**Why price targets liquidity:** large participants need volume, resting orders, and willing counterparties — liquidity provides all three. So price sweeps highs before reversing, or takes lows before moving up, or breaks levels briefly and rejects. Price isn't faking you out — it's seeking liquidity because that's what it's trained to do.

**Liquidity sweep:** price moves into a liquidity pool, triggers stop-losses, then reacts/changes behavior. A sweep does NOT automatically mean reversal — sometimes price sweeps and continues. Context always matters.

**Liquidity ≠ direction.** Liquidity alone gives no direction — it's a DESTINATION, not an entry; a REASON, not a signal. Direction comes from higher-timeframe bias and chart structure (other lessons). Beginner mistakes to avoid: "price hit liquidity so it must reverse" (false); "every high sweep is manipulation" (false); "liquidity is a trade setup" (false — it helps within a setup, not as the whole decision). Liquidity explains WHY price moved, not HOW to trade it.

**Best practice:** higher timeframe is always best for liquidity — use 1H or 4H for accurate liquidity levels. Homework: mark equal highs/lows on past charts, watch how price behaves around them.

### Session Liquidity  (youtu.be/MMeiHtXCALg)
Types of liquidity — "the profitability pillar": your daily bias can be wrong and your strategy mid, but if your draws on liquidity are strong you'll be profitable. Pair a strong strategy with strong draws on liquidity and you win the majority of trades. First type: basic **session highs and session lows**. To see sessions in TradingView, add the indicator **"ICT Killzones Toolkit by LuxAlgo"** — it marks New York AM, New York PM, London, and Asia sessions. The difference between sessions is just which banks control the market at that time (NY AM = 8:30 CT / 9:30 ET American banks; London = London banks; Asia = Asian banks — same chart, different controllers at different times). Mark out session highs/lows that have NOT been retested (if price already ran through a level, it's no longer a valid draw). Off New York open, price aims to seek one of those untested levels. Liquidity is the fuel/gas; price is the car — the car can't move without fuel. Knowing how to mark these out improves trading a ton.

### Swing Highs n Lows  (youtu.be/5_iAo1cyCpw)
Second type of liquidity: **swing highs and swing lows** — the same as a session high/low except NOT inside an actual session (e.g. a low between the Asia and London sessions that price has never returned to). Just because it's not in a session doesn't mean it's not valid. When marking highs/lows, do NOT use the 1-minute chart — almost always use 1H or 15m (4H also fine). 1m is great for ENTRIES, but for liquidity levels zoom out to 15m / 1H / 4H to cut out the junk candles — those are the only three timeframes you'll ever need for liquidity. Validity check: mark a swing low between sessions, then go to the 1H to confirm it's still valid. Note: a swing low that sits INSIDE a fair value gap is weaker (less strong). A valid swing high = an untested higher high outside a session; a valid swing low = same on the downside (sell-side liquidity). This type of liquidity is very important for understanding high-quality liquidity (next video).

### Trade Breakdown 3.13.26 (COVENANT CHECKLIST GUIDE)  (youtube.com/watch?v=5Goi5mEU0zs)
Good afternoon everybody. We are back with another trade breakdown — an insane, perfect trade — but this also doubles as a video on how to use the new Covenant Model checklist in the server.

This blue up-close candle is where market opened (8:30 Central / 9:30 Eastern) — the market open candle. Going back to the 15-minute: before market opened around 7:45, we made **equal highs** — two highs at the exact same level, creating equal highs. Look at equal highs as another level of **liquidity** (same as a session high or valid session low). Liquidity acts like a magnet that pulls price toward it (like planets have gravity). So when I saw the equal highs form, I marked them immediately and knew we'd most likely retrace to them at some point in the AM session, because it's one of the nearest draws on liquidity.

What actually happened: market opens, we accumulate for a bit (small candle bodies, long wicks), then we **displace lower** (manipulation), forming a fair value gap (regular FVG). Then those wicks/bodies hit a level — on the 1-hour there's a **1-hour fair value gap** (a higher-timeframe gap / PD array). That 1H gap acts as support and won't let price close below it. Then we get a huge **distribution** leg up to the equal highs, and we take that entire distribution leg. Why can we take it? Because the regular FVG became an **IFVG** (inverse fair value gap) — it got inversed by the up-close candle. So we take entry there, **stop below the IFVG, target the equal highs**, and bang out (~3R, over two "bandos"). Textbook covenant model: accumulation → manipulation → distribution. We caught the distribution leg because of the IFVG, and confluence from how well price held at the 1H FVG.

**How to use the checklist:**
1. **Did we sweep liquidity first?** What liquidity did we sweep before entry? Higher-timeframe gaps (1H, 4H gaps = PD arrays) can also act as liquidity — levels of support/resistance, which is a level of liquidity. We tap into the gap and collect stops.
2. **Are we delivering from a higher-timeframe level?** Here we have a 1H gap — a higher-timeframe level. "Delivering" = where are we bouncing/coming out of for the distribution. Here we deliver off the 1H FVG.
3. **Displacement** — inside the manipulation leg, was there strong displacement / an actual move? Both times: we make the FVG with strong momentum to the downside, and when we deliver off the 1H FVG we get a huge up-close candle that inverses the entire gap. Strong displacement on both sides. (If we never got that downside FVG, we'd never be able to enter.)
4. **Was there a fair value gap inversion — and were ALL the gaps in the manipulation leg inversed?** Count the gaps in the leg; here there's one gap; did all get inversed? Yes — check.
5. **Is there a clear draw on liquidity for our take-profit?** We must exit somewhere — that's the take-profit level. Our TP matches the equal highs we marked (liquidity is a magnet). Nearest draw = equal highs; next would be the higher-timeframe gap. If equal highs weren't there, drag TP to the bottom of the gap. Examples of clear draws on liquidity: equal highs/lows, session highs/lows, higher-timeframe FVGs, internal liquidity pools (swing highs/lows), pre-market higher-timeframe liquidity. **If there's no clear draw, we don't take the trade** (or take a 1:1 if everything else checks off, or skip entirely).

**Quick formula:** Liquidity Sweep → Displacement → Inversion → Expansion (→ target a clear draw on liquidity).
Walkthrough: market opens → go down to liquidity level = sweep → displacement (gap created) → inversion (ALL gaps inversed) → expansion (we get it, even past the equal highs).

**Golden rule:** Most losing traders see ANY inversion and enter, then wonder why it fails. Covenant traders only take it when it's **obvious**: liquidity sweep, displacement, inversion, then expansion, targeting a clear draw on liquidity. If it's not obvious, it's not the Covenant Model. This is the 4–5 step entry model: liquidity sweep (e.g. tapping a 1H FVG) → displacement (FVG created on the down leg) → inversion (up-close candle inverses that FVG) → clear draw on liquidity for the take-profit (equal highs).

Recap: accumulation → manipulation (tap 1H FVG) → inversion FVG (IFVG) → target equal highs. You only need: a nice liquidity sweep, strong displacement on the manipulation leg, a valid inversion (all or most gaps in the leg inversed), and a clear draw on liquidity to know when to take profit.

**Extra rule (from Nicholas):** Delivery must come from EITHER a liquidity sweep OR a 15-minute-or-higher FVG (15m / 1H / 4H FVG / PD array).

## ADVANCED EXECUTION › heatmaps

### Aggressive Liquidity  (youtu.be/11Drn5skje0)
Aggressive vs passive liquidity. **Aggressive liquidity = market orders** — entering at the current price level. This is what the Covenant Model uses (our orders are aggressive — we enter at current price). Aggressive liquidity creates visible candlesticks: a bullish candle = more buys than sells; a down (gray) leg = more sellers than buyers. Important: with aggressive liquidity you can only see PREVIOUS filled orders (via past candles) and what's currently happening (the current candle) — you can't see the future. That's the only info candlesticks give you. (Tip of the iceberg for liquidity.)

### Passive Liquidity  (youtu.be/3HbQv8rs68w)
**Passive liquidity = stop orders or limit orders** — orders outside of candlesticks / outside the current price, waiting to be filled (resting, not active yet). Example: setting "enter me long if price hits up here" or "enter me short if price hits down here" — you're not in yet. On a normal candlestick chart you can ONLY see your OWN passive orders — you can't see anyone else's resting orders. That's a problem because ~99% of market liquidity is passive — big banks, institutions, and hedge funds placing huge orders at certain levels waiting to be filled. The next step (heatmaps) shows how to actually SEE other people's passive liquidity on the chart (like "wall hacks").

### Reading Heatmaps  (youtu.be/k0sndsZ6oN4)
The big surprise: you CAN see other people's passive orders — using the **heatmap** ("our version of Call of Duty wall hacks" — a deeper view with more info). Heatmaps show liquidity invisible to 99% of traders, like institutional limit orders (e.g. where a big bank like JP Morgan placed orders). On the heatmap, liquidity is shown as **yellow thick lines** — the thicker the line, the more liquidity at that level. What makes a liquidity level VALID/important: it must touch the live "deck of numbers" (the DOM/price ladder on the right). If a level used to be active but is no longer touching those numbers, someone canceled their order — it's no longer valid. So out of all the yellow on a chart, only the clusters/lines that are currently active (touching the numbers) matter; the rest is previous liquidity already swept or cancelled.

### What are All Time Highs  (youtu.be/Hs9qOZRu9qw)
How to trade all-time highs (ATH) vs a regular market. ATH = a price NQ has never been at before — the highest NQ has ever been in its lifetime. Identify it by zooming out to the higher timeframe: price goes up, plateaus, up, plateaus (the 4-hour plateaus are actually dips on the lower timeframe). The perfect time to take a BUY in ATH is during the DIP. So identify the dips and ride the distribution in all-time highs. During ATH, do NOT take shorts unless it's literally right at 8:30 Central / 9:30 Eastern (market open).

### Spotting An ATH Entry  (youtu.be/-HbRiTFkZwY)
How to identify and execute ATH dip-buy trades. To recognize which dips are valid (and will bounce and continue distributing higher): identify a **gap in pre-market on a decent timeframe** — not a 5-minute; look for **15m, 30m, 1H, or 4H** (the higher the timeframe, the stronger). Then wait for price to do its thing, go above, and **revisit that gap** — and take a **lower-timeframe inversion**. Important distinction: a dip only qualifies if there's a **fair value gap to get inversed**. If a dip into the gap has NO fair value gap, it's weak confirmation — skip it (he held off on a dip into a 15m gap that had no FVG, and instead waited for another dip that formed a gap; right when that gap got inversed, he entered). Summary: higher timeframe creates a gap → price revisits the gap → lower timeframe gives an inversion to the upside → entry.

### How To TP During ATH  (youtu.be/D-8ju5xQOl4)
Example entry: dipped into the gap → created a fair value gap (black candle) → inversed it (blue candle) → took the inversion and rode it to the previous high. The TP problem at ATH: there is **no buy-side liquidity to target** above (we're at new highs). When there's no buy-side liquidity to target, **target the previous swing high**, and **take partials at internal highs** on the way up (internal highs are even better if they're fair value gaps). So: target the very top of the last swing high and scale out partials as you reach internal highs to lock in profit.

### Staying Profitable During ATH  (youtu.be/jbkd3qNwYWA)
At all-time highs you do NOT rely completely on the Covenant Model — it's a variation (still accumulation → manipulation → inversion, but it looks a bit different because NQ has never been at this price). Anticipate that price keeps pushing higher until one day you get a **break of structure to the downside** on the higher timeframe (1H or 4H). Until that break of structure, it just keeps pumping. **At ATH it is NOT a good idea to take shorts — do not do it; you'll lose (and if you win it's luck).** Only take LONGS at all-time highs. Exception: during the intra-session New York AM open (8:30 CT / 9:30 ET) NQ can do whatever it wants — long or short is fine right at the open depending on direction. But once you're trading the NY session past the open, only look for long setups: let NQ dip back into a significant gap, wait for an inversion, then take your entry. That's how you trade ATH and stay profitable.

### Protected Stop Losses In Depth  (youtu.be/IUlao1iFpuA)
Protected vs unprotected stop-loss. Many beginners have the right trade (right Covenant Model direction) but still get stopped out because they place stops in the wrong spot.

**The core principle: your stop-loss should be placed at the point where your trade idea is INVALIDATED.** For a long (trying to go higher), the market must make higher lows; the one thing that invalidates a long is a **lower low**. So your stop goes below the level that, if broken, proves you wrong — the **swing low of the manipulation leg**. (For a short, it's the opposite — invalidated by a higher high.) As long as price doesn't drop below that swing low, the idea is still valid.

**Don't put the stop just below the first fair value gap** — a wick often pushes through the FVG even when the candle body doesn't close through it, so you get wicked out of a good trade. The smarter stop is at the swing low of the manipulation leg (which the Covenant Model teaches).

**"Lines of defense":** think of each gap/level as a line of defense protecting your stop. More lines = harder to get stopped out. Example: FVG #1 = line of defense 1, FVG #2 = line of defense 2, swing low = line of defense 3. Prefer placing the stop beyond ALL the gaps down to the swing low, getting the **most lines of defense possible while RR is still ≥ 1:1**. Do NOT shrink the stop to just below the first gap merely to get better RR (e.g. 1.7R) — you'd throw away a free line of defense the market handed you.

**Trade off market structure, not purely RR.** Good RR is nice, but place stops AND take-profits based on market structure/data — that's when trades actually win and you can let runners ride. If your trade ideas are correct but you keep getting stopped out, switch from arbitrary stops to structure-based stops (statistically much harder to get stopped out with two gaps protecting your stop vs one). If you can still get 1:1 by going to the swing low, go to the swing low and give yourself breathing room. Once price has retraced and you're ~halfway to TP, it's okay to move the stop to reduce risk.
